## What you'll learn

This is the first lesson in our adaptation of [Stanford CME 295: Transformers & Large Language Models](https://cme295.stanford.edu/), Lecture 1. The full Stanford lecture covers a lot of ground: NLP basics, tokenization, embeddings, RNNs, attention, the full transformer architecture. We've broken it into a small course so each idea gets the room it deserves, and this is the first lesson in that course. It is the heart of the lecture and the heart of the whole architecture: self-attention. It answers one question: when an AI reads a sentence, how does it figure out which words go with which? We're going to build the intuition without burying you in linear algebra, work through one attention score by hand, and stop before the math swallows the meaning.

## Before you start

**Prerequisites:** none from Clawdemy yet. You don't need any prior ML background. If you're rusty on what a dot product does, watch [3Blue1Brown's "Dot products and duality"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LyGKycYT2v0) (about 14 minutes) before you start. It's the one piece of math intuition the lesson assumes, and the rest of linear algebra we'll explain inline as we need it.

## By the end, you'll be able to

- Explain in plain language what attention does and why it replaced the sequence-by-sequence approach RNNs used
- Distinguish self-attention from cross-attention by what each one is comparing
- Decompose the attention formula into the role each of its three inputs (query, key, value) plays in producing one score
- Run the attention computation by hand on a small worked matrix of three tokens

## Time and difficulty

- **Read time:** about 25 minutes
- **Practice time:** about 20 minutes (a worked attention computation on paper, plus flashcards)
- **Difficulty:** standard
